Frequently Asked Questions

1. Whence the gradual transition from ecologist to data scientist to project manager/system designer/software engineer?

I wanted a career that satiates and better integrates the logical and creative hemispheres of my brain. There's not much difference between the binary and biological arts. Both involve research and innovation in their respective fields. Both maintain a precarious three way balance between problem solving, content generation, and interdisciplinary collaboration. Both generate and test hypotheses, discover intriguing patterns in the world around us, and model complex systems using mathematics.

2. What sort of person am I?

In my experience, people in an academic or business environment act like foraging song birds. I consider myself a robin with a generous helping of crow and a smidgeon of blue jay to temper the mix. Perhaps a bit of bunting in my dreams, but I tend to be too risk adverse. What sort are you?

  1. Blue Jay: Drown out others with your glorious voice. Push everyone aside with your mighty wings. Then claim your food.

  2. Bunting: Where others see clearcut trees as ravaged desolation, you see an opportunity to advance into virgin territory.

  3. Cowbird: Foist the responsibility of your fledgling ideas on others. Then lecture them on how you would have raised that chick.

  4. Crow: Keep testing new methods to break that clam. Beak. Rock. Car tire. Gravity. The world is your toolbox.

  5. Magpie: Get distracted by the newest shiny thing. Bring it back to your nest, discarding all others. Then shit on it.

  6. Robin: Plan your worm hunt in detail. Rise early the next day to search the entire field. Agonize over which worm is the best.

4. What is my project management philosophy?

  • Think all things through completely; solicit all input humbly; accept all feedback gracefully
  • Establish unambiguous spheres of responsibility, a schedule, and concrete milestones for every task you seek to implement
  • Planning, building, and managing the same task each require different mindsets and skillsets
  • Debrief after every project to redress potential flaws in the next project
  • Pre and post production phases are important...don't neglect them
  • Encourage lower echelon ingenuity: upper level employees decide what needs to get done while lower level employees should be trusted to plan how and adapt that plan as needed
  • While one group update meeting (i.e., stand meeting) per week is necessary, once per week is quite enough...and provide some comfortable chairs and a nice, round table

6. What are my thoughts on the iterative parallel project planning (i.e., agile) vs sequential series project planing (i.e., waterfall) debate?

There should be more debate, actually. Agile has spread like kudzu, smothering everything that came before it. I support adaptation, but using a pure agile methodology and forsaking waterfall throws the baby out with the bathwater. Useful things like robust planning and evaluation, design documentation, and well-defined endpoints are tossed aside. Using a hybrid approach that takes the best from both worlds is better. So, I use waterfall for large scale project components like scheduling and agile for small scale components like feature development with a kanban board intermediary.

7. Why is the post project debrief so important?

Many stop and pause before beginning a project to plan (i.e., kickoff meeting), but few likewise pause afterwards to reflect (i.e., after action debrief). This is a costly mistake. How can we improve subsequent projects if we do not address what worked and what did not—and more importantly discuss why—while the recent finished one is still fresh in our minds?

8. What is my system design philosophy?

  • Follow the 7P's: Plan, Prototype, Playtest, Polish, Patch, Publish, Post
  • Make your project accessible because not everyone sees, hears, or interprets symobls and stimuli the same way that you do
  • Never embrace the esoteric at the expense of the fundamentals
  • Create the mechanics first, the necessary functions second, and the aesthetics third
  • Build your product to flex and evolve
  • Make it modular, then reuse your assets
  • Decouple everything (i.e. separate concerns)
  • Our life is frittered away by detail. Simplify, simplify, simplify!

9. How do I avoid scope creep and endgame system bloat?

I priotizize system feature implementation into three tiers—minimum, core, and wishlist—at the onset. Brainstorm and then prune heavily: most proposed features will (and should) be wishlisted. Then don't even glance at subsequent tiers until every feature in the prior one is finished, tested, and polished.

10. Who is the best author for system design documentation?

Everyone! Each individual working on a proposed system needs to contribute their input on its design. Such documents should always be short, adaptable, and collaborative.

11. Why is designing for accessibility so important?

Not only does designing for a wide and varied user base allow you to court a larger audience, you also take advantage of positive unintended consequences (i.e., the curb cut effect). Making your program easy to ue and dynamically adjustable to suit different needs will cast beneficial ripples beyond the intended core audience.

12. What is my code writing philosophy?

  • Hardcode as little as possible
  • Always favor composition over inheritance
  • Give your variables and functions descriptive names
  • Each function should do one thing and one thing well
  • In the tradeoff between fewer functions (optimize!) and more functions (specialize!), err towards the latter
  • Nested if else statements are evil incarnate, nested for loops hordes of little demonspawn, and if/elseif chains their intruments of doom
  • Split your code into independently testable bite sized chunks—do not make a giant slimy block of spaghetti code lest you choke on it
  • Plan with the mindset that you or someone else will need to modify, rearrange, or update this code someday
  • If someone who does not know the language in which you are coding can still interpret what your code is supposed to do, you have succeeded

14. Why do I rarely comment my code?

"Elegant code is like elegant dialogue," he said. Less is more. Exhaustive detail doesn't make things better, it's a crutch. Most comments are an excuse to handwave disorganized code, ambiguous variable names, or overcomplex functions. One should be able to read good code like the pages of a good book, never mind the footnotes. The sole exceptions are for cases of instruction or categorical organization.

15. What is my animus against using frameworks?

Other than that they result in bloated, slow, dependency-ridden code that adds unnecesary layers of abstraction, limits applicability, and makes porting tooth-pulling difficult, I have no specific problem with frameworks. They drift in and out of fashion like clothes or memes and the vaniila code remains strong.
